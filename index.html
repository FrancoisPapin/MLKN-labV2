Knowledge Without Borders: The Epistemic Case for Interdisciplinarity

A Scientific Perspective · MLKN.lab · May 2026

Interdisciplinarity ontologies across the 26 disciplines—organized into 5 Core Discipline Domains (Formal Sciences, Natural Sciences, Health Sciences, Social Sciences, Humanities, Applied Sciences)— stratify into five ontological layers for MultiLayer Knowledge Network (MLKN) frameworks. Layer 1 (Core Discipline Domains) establishes the highest-level classification, following OECD Frascati and UNESCO standards. Layer 2 (Academic Disciplines) represents the 26 established academic fields, aligned with Scopus and Web of Science classifications. Layer 3 (Academic Subdisciplines) captures specialized subfields (e.g., AI within Computer Science, Cognitive Neuroscience within Neuroscience), aligned with Scopus Sub-Areas and UNESCO 6-digit fields. Layer 4 (Core Thematic Domains) organizes thematic clusters within subdisciplines (e.g., Machine Learning within AI, Brain Imaging within Cognitive Neuroscience), representing research topics and cognitive science frameworks. Layer 5 (Main Thematics) includes fundamental thematics and concepts (e.g., Supervised Learning within Machine Learning, fMRI within Brain Imaging), aligned with Scopus Keywords, MeSH Terms, and IEEE Thesaurus.

These layers are complemented by four epistemological meta-layers: Epistemic Roles (how knowledge is generated/validated, following NSF and OECD classifications), Levels of Analysis (scale at which phenomena are studied, following WHO, CDC, and Systems Theory standards), Paradigms (philosophical assumptions, based on Kuhn and Lakatos), and Standards Alignment (alignment with OECD Frascati, UNESCO, Scopus, MeSH, and IEEE classifications). Weighted bridges (major ≥4: philosophy–neuroscience; minor 2–3: anthropology–computer science) visualize thematic flows in MLKN graphs, revealing intra-cluster density and inter-cluster sparsity (Buccella, Maoz, & Mudrik, 2024; Muscolino et al., 2022).

Bridge Epistemologies

Epistemological bridges hybridize roles and scales, yielding critical gains amid tensions. Philosophy's foundational role bridges neuroscience's empirical molecular–neural data, as in coevolutionary "science of the mind" models integrating qualia debates with fMRI findings (Buccella, Maoz, & Mudrik, 2024; Newen et al., 2024). Methodological computer science enables anthropological social–cultural analysis (e.g., network algorithms for human rights narratives), while systems science's theoretical frames link environmental global scales to education's applied individual pedagogies (Alasehir & Acarturk, 2022; Zum & Bassett, 2020).

Critically, bridges expose epistemic frictions: normative human rights challenges environmental science's empirical universalism, demanding minor-weighted reconciliations (Adams, 2007). Trends favor methodological–empirical synergies (cognitive psychology–language science at individual scales), addressing nexus problems like cultural biases in AI (computer science–anthropology) (Okamura, 2025). Such integrations amplify validity, as Doc2Vec analyses confirm cognitive science's balanced psychology–philosophy–linguistics ties (Alasehir & Acarturk, 2022; Cai, Lyu, & Zhou, 2023).

Nexus Dynamics in 5-Layer Frameworks

Nexus dynamics crystallize at layer intersections, driving thematic interdisciplinarity and big research nexuses in MLKN frameworks. Primary nexuses in the 5-layer structure: (1) Formal–Natural Nexus (Mathematical Science–Computer Science ↔ Neuroscience–Environmental Science–Biology), where abstract models meet empirical data; (2) Health–Social Nexus (Medicine–Public Health–Bioethics ↔ Anthropology–Law–Education Science), linking clinical practice with societal impacts; (3) Cognitive–Technological Nexus (Neuroscience–Psychology ↔ Computer Science–Systems Science), fusing understanding of mind with intelligent systems; (4) Techno–Ethical–Sustainability Nexus (Computer Science–Engineering–Design ↔ Philosophy–Human Rights–Environmental Science), addressing global challenges through technology and ethics; (5) Adaptive Learning Nexus (Education Science–Psychology ↔ Systems Science–Computer Science), developing personalized learning systems.

Emerging trends include graph analytics quantifying distant citations (computer science enabling network analysis) and crisis-responsive thematics (climate cognition: neuroscience–environmental science) (Krenn et al., 2023; Rong, Chen, Ma, & Koch, 2025). The 5-layer structure reveals both the depth of specialization within disciplines and the breadth of connections between them, with big problems persisting in under-connected areas (Schwartz, 2021). Dynamics reveal overt interdisciplinarity in cognitive fields, with linguistics–psychology majors evolving post-2016 (Alasehir & Acarturk, 2022; Baaden, Rennings, John, & Bröring, 2024; Okamura, 2019).

Prospective Trajectories in Multilayer Networks

Prospective trajectories pivot on institutional scaffolds for role–scale balance in multilayer knowledge networks, forecasting "nexus ontologies" by 2030. Emerging trends: AI-augmented graphs (computer science–systems science) dynamically weighting bridges across layers; normative escalations (human rights–philosophy informing environmental AI ethics at global scales); trans-scale methodological hubs (language–education for multimodal learning across analysis levels) (Marwitz et al., 2026; Krenn, 2023; Zum & Bassett, 2020).

Critically, trajectories hinge on resolving evaluation biases—interdisciplinary works under-cited despite higher impacts—via role-explicit funding (Xiang, Romero, & Teplitsky, 2025; Park, Maity, Wutchy, & Wang, 2026). Big nexus expansions in the 5-layer framework: eco-neuro-anthropology on planetary cognition (Natural Sciences ↔ Social Sciences ↔ Humanities); digital human rights via anthropology–computer science (Social Sciences ↔ Formal Sciences); bioethics of AI in healthcare (Health Sciences ↔ Formal Sciences ↔ Humanities). While raw empiricism risks becoming an isolated hegemony, the integration of normative inquiry ensures a balanced trajectory; it is this coevolutionary dialogue—signaled by the philosophy–neuroscience nexus—that promises a genuine epistemic renaissance (Buccella, Maoz, & Mudrik, 2024; Wuchty, Jones, & Uzzi, 2007).
